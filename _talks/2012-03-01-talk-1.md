Human-forest interactions support the livelihoods of nearly one quarter of the global population, yet their responses to climate variability remain poorly understood. Here, we develop a global framework integrating forest quality change and human activity gradients to quantify coupling sensitivity (D*). We show that 77.7% of global forests declined in quality from 2000 to 2020, with strong spatial heterogeneity across four interaction regimes. Ecological improvement regions exhibit the highest sensitivity despite limited extent. We found that climate stability, rather than mean climate conditions, reshapes human–forest systems by amplifying coupling through indirect pathways linking human activity and forest dynamics. Under stable climates, internal social–ecological feedbacks dominate system behavior, while external forcing prevails under variable conditions. Nonlinear analyses reveal threshold effects, indicating a “climate stability window” that maximizes system responsiveness. These results identify climate stability as a key regulator of human–forest coupling and provide insights for climate-adaptive forest management.
